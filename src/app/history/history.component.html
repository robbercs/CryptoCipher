Cryptography has played a crucial role throughout human history. Since the dawn of time, people have sought ingenious ways to protect sensitive information and ensure the confidentiality of communication. Starting with rudimentary forms of encryption used by ancient civilizations and evolving into the most complex algorithms of the current digital era, this practice has exerted a profound influence on our society.

1900 a.C

Tomb of Khnumhotep II

The first known use of encryption comes from an inscription carved on the chamber of the tomb of Khnumhotep II, a noble from ancient Egypt. In this case, it is believed that the scribe chose to use unusual hieroglyphs instead of the more conventional ones, not to hide the message itself but probably to give it a more dignified appearance.

Caesar Cipher

In the times of the Romans, we have the example of the well-known Caesar Cipher. This cipher is a substitution cipher, as it involves taking each letter of the plaintext and shifting it k times in the alphabet, thereby obtaining an encrypted text. For example, for a key k = 3, the letter 'a' would become 'd', and the letter 'c' would become 'f'. This cipher system can be better understood through two wheels containing the entire alphabet.

100 a.C

XV-XVI

Vigenere Cipher

One of the early polyalphabetic ciphers is the Vigenere Cipher. Unlike the Caesar Cipher, where a simple shift of characters is performed, in this cipher, each letter in the plaintext is shifted by different positions according to a keyword. The keyword is repeated throughout the message for encrypting and decrypting the text. The letter at the corresponding position of the keyword indicates how many positions the corresponding letter of the plaintext should be shifted.

Enigma Machine

The emergence of polyalphabetic encryption responded to the need for security in communications, especially during the turn of the century with the rise of radio transmissions. In the case of the Enigma, its rotors and cables generated a dynamic and changing encryption for each letter, making it highly intricate and difficult to decipher. Despite the complexity of machines like the Enigma, the British managed to decipher crucial messages through collaboration with Polish cryptographers and the genius of Alan Turing, contributing significantly to the Allied war effort.
